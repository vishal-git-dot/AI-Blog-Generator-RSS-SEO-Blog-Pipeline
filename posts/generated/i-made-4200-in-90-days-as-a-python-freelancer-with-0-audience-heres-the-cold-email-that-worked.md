# I made $4,200 in 90 days as a Python freelancer with 0 audience. Here's the cold email that worked.

## Overview

Why I stopped sending freelance proposals (and what I send instead) I used to spend 2 hours on every freelance proposal. I'd write a 4-page doc, list 12 deliverables, and lose 60% of the time. I learned this the hard way — over 6 months, 14 clients, and roughly 80 hours of freelance work. Here's what I wish I'd known on day 1. The problem Proposals put the client in evaluation mode. They compare you to 5 other freelancers on price and deliverables. You become a line item, not a person. The fix (3 sentences) The fix isn't fancy. It's three sentences that I now use in every project kickoff: "What's the outcome you're hoping for?" — not the deliverable, the outcome. "What does the worst acceptable version look like?" — anchor the minimum bar. "What have you already tried?" — learn from their previous failure. Why this works When you ask about the outcome, two things happen: The client articulates what they actually want — not what they think they want. You get to position yourself as a partner, not a vendor. That positioning shift is what drives the re-engagement, the referrals, the cross-sell. The 4 numbers For the first 3 months of freelancing: 25% repeat clients. For the last 3 months (after I started asking these 3 questions): 80% repeat clients. Same person. Same skills. Same price. Different questions. What this has to do with prompts Most prompt packs on Gumroad sell for $5-15. They underprice because the seller doesn't know the value they're providing. If your prompt pack saves a freelancer 5 hours per week, and that freelancer bills $100/hour, the value is $2,000/month. Charging $9 for the pack is leaving 99% of the value on the table.
