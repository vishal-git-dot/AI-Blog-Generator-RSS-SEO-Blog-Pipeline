# How I Use Automation to Track NQ Futures Reversal Levels

## Overview

Most futures traders are not short on opinions. They are short on a repeatable process. That is the problem I have been working on with PlayMaker: turning my futures trading workflow into something more structured, trackable, and easier to review. This is not about replacing trade judgment. It is about organizing the data around a setup so a trader can slow down, check risk, and avoid chasing candles. The problem When trading Nasdaq futures, price can move fast. A level may look clean, then price may run through it, retrace, or react before the trader has time to think through the setup. The questions I want answered before a limit order are simple: Is this level near an area where price could realistically react? Is the setup aligned with higher-timeframe context? Is there enough confluence to justify the risk? Can the trade be managed with a light stop, usually around 7-15 points? If there are multiple nearby levels, should risk be split instead of stacked too heavily at one price? What PlayMaker is designed to help with PlayMaker is built around futures market workflow, especially NQ futures setups. It helps track automatic signal results, manual setup grading, journal stats, and order-card style levels. The goal is not to tell somebody to blindly take every trade. The goal is to show where price could likely react for a high-of-day or low-of-day opportunity, then help the trader review the setup before execution. A good setup still needs risk management. I like keeping risk light with 7-15 point stops when possible and reviewing whether price is respecting the level, rejecting, wicking, or showing signs that the idea is wrong. Why automation helps Manual notes are useful, but they are easy to lose or forget. Automation makes it easier to: keep setup cards organized track wins, losses, and break-even results review daily and weekly points compare manual trade ideas against automatic signal results avoid emotional decisions when price is moving fast The biggest value is consistency. If the same kind of trade is logged the same way every time, the journal becomes useful instead of just being a list of random notes. What I am tracking Some of the main things I care about are: entry level direction max move drawdown result notes whether a setup was verified how the trade behaved after entry Over time, this helps answer better questions. Not just “did this trade win?” but “did this type of level usually give enough reaction to justify the stop?” Final thought For me, trading tools should make the process cleaner. They should help a trader review risk, wait for stronger levels, and learn from the data after the trade is over. That is what I am building with PlayMaker.
