# How to Balance SMB Workloads Between Cloud and Colocation

## Overview

Many small and medium-sized businesses (SMBs) ask the same question: which workloads belong in the public cloud, and which should remain on dedicated infrastructure? The answer is simple: keep predictable, heavy workloads on fixed infrastructure in a secure colocation facility, and use public cloud resources for apps with fluctuating demands. For most Irvine businesses, a hybrid approach is ideal for balancing costs, scalability, performance, and operational flexibility. If you are looking to balance cloud with colocation, you are in the right place. A proper split will help keep performance predictable, costs fixed, and sensitive data under your control. Below is a guide on how to build that split, common mistakes to avoid, and a side-by-side look at when each environment earns its place in your stack. Understanding The Basics Before figuring out how to split your SMB workload between the public cloud and a private colocation center, it helps to know what each term actually means. What Is Public Cloud? Public cloud allows you to rent computing resources from large cloud providers such as Amazon Web Services (AWS) or Microsoft Azure. You can rapidly deploy servers, apps, storage, and networking without investing in hardware. Benefits include: Fast deployment Rapid scalability Flexible resource allocation Pay-as-you-go pricing Global accessibility Public clouds are well-suited for marketing sites, apps with variable traffic, or environments with short-lived demands. However, you should be wary of an "all-in-the-cloud" mindset. Some businesses migrate every database, file, and application to AWS or Azure, only to be hit by unusually high monthly bills. So, the smartest strategy is not to choose just cloud but to combine it with colocation. What Is Colocation? Colocation is having your own servers, storage, and networking gear physically sit in a professionally managed, third-party data center. Your colocation partner provides cooling, redundant power, security, bandwidth, and on-site engineering support, while you retain physical control and enjoy predictable costs and performance. How to Decide Where Each Workload Belongs When evaluating workloads, do not assume every application belongs in the cloud. Some applications benefit from the flexibility of public cloud resources, while others perform better on dedicated infrastructure. Placing every workload in the right infrastructure builds a more effective IT landscape. Consider Colocation For: Core business operations Stable monthly resource usage Apps demanding predictable performance and stricter compliance or security control Direct engineer access to hardware or infrastructure In particular, workloads that perform better on dedicated infrastructure include: Databases (SQL/NoSQL) with steady I/O File storage repositories Backup systems ERP platforms Historical file archives Accounting systems Compliance-sensitive applications Line-of-business apps Customer portals These systems generally consume a consistent amount of resources, and colocation ensures that performance is not affected by neighboring tenants.
