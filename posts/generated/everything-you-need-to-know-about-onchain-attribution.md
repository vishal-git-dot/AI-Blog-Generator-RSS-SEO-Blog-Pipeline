# Everything You Need to Know About Onchain Attribution

## Overview

Marketing agencies managing crypto and DeFi clients face a measurement problem that has no equivalent in traditional digital marketing: the users they're trying to reach don't have cookies, don't log in with email addresses, and interact with products through pseudonymous wallet addresses. Without a framework designed for this environment, campaign reporting stays stuck at impressions and clicks — metrics that tell you almost nothing about whether your strategy is actually moving protocol liquidity, generating NFT mints, or growing a DeFi protocol's active user base. Onchain attribution is the answer, and understanding how it works is now a baseline requirement for any agency serving Web3 clients. Key Takeaways Onchain attribution uses wallet addresses as persistent user identifiers, replacing browser cookies that don't exist in the blockchain environment. UTM parameters must be preserved through the wallet connection event to link marketing sources to on-chain actions that happen later. The metrics that matter in Web3 are verifiable: transaction volume, wallet connection rate, and protocol liquidity moved — not clicks or impressions. Agencies that can connect campaigns to actual fund movement retain clients longer because they're demonstrating provable ROI, not speculative reach. Onchain data is immutable and publicly verifiable, which means attribution claims can be audited — a level of transparency Web2 analytics cannot match. Adopting an onchain attribution framework repositions marketing spend from an unverified cost center to a data-driven growth engine. What Onchain Attribution Is — and Why It's Different from Web2 In traditional digital marketing, attribution means tracing a conversion back to the ad, email, or search result that drove it. The plumbing relies on browser cookies, device fingerprints, and login sessions — all of which assume a persistent, identifiable user operating inside a browser session that your tracking scripts can observe. Web3 breaks every one of those assumptions. When a user connects a wallet to a DeFi protocol or NFT marketplace, they're not authenticating with an email address. They're presenting a cryptographic public key. There's no browser session to attach a cookie to across the entire interaction with the protocol's smart contracts — the meaningful actions happen on-chain, outside the DOM entirely. Onchain attribution reframes the measurement problem around what Web3 actually provides: a public, immutable ledger of wallet interactions. Instead of asking "which cookie session led to this conversion?", onchain attribution asks "which wallet address took this action, and which marketing touchpoint can we tie to that wallet?" The wallet address becomes the persistent user identifier — one that exists independently of any browser, device, or session. This is a fundamentally more durable identity signal. A wallet address doesn't expire, doesn't get cleared when a user switches browsers, and doesn't disappear behind iOS privacy prompts. The trade-off is that it's pseudonymous by default, which means attribution requires deliberate instrumentation at the point where Web2 marketing channels meet on-chain user behavior. Why Cookie-Based Tracking Fails in Web3 Most Web3 projects start by bolting Google Analytics onto their landing pages and calling it attribution. This works for measuring blog traffic and email click-throughs, but it breaks down completely the moment a user leaves the browser context and interacts with a smart contract. Consider the journey of a typical DeFi user: they click a paid social ad, land on a protocol's website, connect a MetaMask wallet, and then — days later — make a deposit from that wallet. The browser cookie that tracked the original click is tied to a session that ended when they closed the tab. The deposit event on-chain has no session context at all. The gap between those two moments is invisible to any tracking system built on cookies alone. Browser-based analytics also can't see multi-wallet behavior. Power users in DeFi routinely operate three to five wallets for different risk profiles. Cookie-based systems count them as separate users and miss the full picture of their protocol engagement. And as Web3 audiences increasingly use hardware wallets, mobile signing apps, and multi-sig setups, the fraction of meaningful activity that happens outside a trackable browser session keeps growing. The result for agencies is a reporting gap that erodes client confidence. If you can't connect your strategy to actual movement of funds, you're always one bad quarter away from losing the account — because the client's internal team can see on-chain activity even if your dashboard can't. How Onchain Attribution Works Technically Closing the gap between marketing touchpoints and on-chain events requires instrumentation at three levels. UTM preservation through wallet connect. When a user arrives from a paid campaign carrying UTM parameters, those parameters need to survive the wallet connection event and get stored — either in a first-party database or passed as metadata to the dApp's backend. The moment a wallet address connects, the system records: wallet address + UTM source + UTM medium + UTM campaign + timestamp. That record is the attribution anchor. Event taxonomy on-chain. Not all on-chain actions are equal. A well-designed attribution model distinguishes between wallet connection (intent), a first transaction (activation), repeat protocol usage (retention), and high-value actions like large deposits or liquidity provision (monetization). Each event tier maps to a different marketing objective. Cross-session wallet resolution. Because a wallet address is constant across sessions, you can match future on-chain activity back to the original attribution record even if weeks pass between the first click and the first meaningful transaction. This is the core advantage over cookie-based systems: the identifier doesn't expire. For agencies, the practical implementation usually involves a lightweight JavaScript snippet on the dApp frontend that fires when eth_requestAccounts resolves, captures the connected address alongside any UTM state from the URL or session storage, and posts that record to an analytics backend. From there, on-chain event data — pulled via RPC or indexed through a subgraph — gets joined to the attribution records by wallet address. Frequently Asked Questions What is onchain attribution and how does it differ from traditional attribution? Onchain attribution is a measurement framework that links marketing touchpoints to blockchain wallet actions, using wallet addresses as persistent user identifiers instead of browser cookies. Traditional attribution depends on cookie sessions, which expire and don't survive the handoff from a browser environment to a smart contract interaction. Onchain attribution closes this gap by anchoring the marketing source to a wallet address at the moment of connection, then joining future on-chain activity back to that record — providing a complete picture of the user journey from ad click to protocol transaction. Why don't cookies work for tracking Web3 users? Browser cookies fail in Web3 because the most meaningful user actions — transactions, liquidity deposits, NFT mints — happen on a blockchain, not inside a browser session that your tracking scripts can observe. A cookie attached to a landing page visit has no connection to a wallet interaction that happens through a signing request. Additionally, DeFi power users frequently operate multiple wallets across different devices and browsers, which means cookie-based systems fragment their behavior into disconnected sessions rather than a unified user profile. How are UTM parameters used in onchain attribution? UTM parameters are captured when a user lands on a dApp from a marketing link, and they must be deliberately preserved through the wallet connection event — typically by reading the URL parameters into session storage and then recording them alongside the wallet address at the moment eth_requestAccounts resolves. This creates an attribution record that ties a specific campaign source to a specific wallet, making it possible to credit future on-chain actions from that wallet back to the original marketing touchpoint even if the user doesn't transact until days or weeks later. What KPIs should marketing agencies report on for Web3 clients? The most meaningful KPIs for Web3 clients are wallet connection rate (what fraction of clicks resulted in a connected wallet), first-transaction rate (what fraction of connected wallets completed at least one on-chain action), transaction volume by campaign source, and total protocol liquidity moved by attributable wallets. These metrics directly reflect the outcomes DeFi protocols care about — user activation and capital inflows — rather than vanity metrics like impressions or raw click counts, which don't distinguish between users who transacted and users who bounced. Can onchain attribution data be independently verified? Yes — onchain attribution is inherently more auditable than Web2 analytics because the underlying transaction data lives on a public blockchain that anyone can query. While the attribution mapping (which wallet connects to which campaign source) lives in a private analytics database, the on-chain half of the equation — whether a wallet transacted, when, and how much — can always be verified against the public ledger. This is a meaningful differentiator for agencies: you can hand a client a wallet address and a block explorer link as evidence for any attributed transaction, which no cookie-based system can replicate. How does onchain attribution help agencies retain Web3 clients? Onchain attribution helps agencies retain clients by replacing speculative reach metrics with verifiable proof that campaigns drove actual protocol activity. When an agency can show that a specific campaign drove a measurable dollar volume of liquidity deposits or protocol transactions, the value conversation shifts from "trust our impressions report" to "here is the on-chain evidence." This closes the credibility gap that causes clients to question marketing ROI — and it gives agencies a clear framework for optimizing spend toward the wallet segments and channels that generate the highest-value users, not just the most traffic.
