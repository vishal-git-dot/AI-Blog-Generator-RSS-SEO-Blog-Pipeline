# How to Calculate Square Footage for Any Room Shape (Rectangle, Circle, Triangle, L-Shape)

## Overview

How to Calculate Square Footage for Any Room Shape (Rectangle, Circle, Triangle, L-Shape) Square footage math looks simple until your room isn't a perfect rectangle. Most online guides only cover length × width, then leave you stuck when you're staring at an L-shaped den or a circular breakfast nook. Here's the actual math for every common room shape, plus the two mistakes that cause people to order the wrong amount of flooring. The four shapes you'll actually run into Rectangle — the easy one: length × width . A 12 ft × 10 ft room is 120 sq ft. Triangle — for triangular alcoves or attic spaces: (base × height) ÷ 2 . A 10 ft base with 8 ft height gives you 40 sq ft, not 80 — this is the step people most often forget. Circle — for round rooms or circular patios: π × (diameter ÷ 2)² . Measure the full diameter, not the radius, then halve it before squaring. A 10 ft diameter circle is π × 5² ≈ 78.5 sq ft. L-shape — split it into two rectangles, calculate each separately, and add them. If your main section is 14 ft × 10 ft (140 sq ft) and the alcove is 6 ft × 8 ft (48 sq ft), the total is 188 sq ft. You can also calculate the full bounding rectangle and subtract the missing corner — both methods land on the same number, so use whichever is easier to measure. Two mistakes that lead to buying the wrong amount of material 1. Forgetting the waste factor. Flooring isn't sold in the exact square footage you need — cuts, offcuts, and pattern matching eat into your material. For a straight-lay installation, add 10% to your total. For diagonal or herringbone patterns, add 15%. A 300 sq ft room needs 330 sq ft of material ordered for a straight lay. Always round up, not down — running short mid-install means a second delivery and a color-lot mismatch risk. 2. Confusing gross and net square footage. Gross square footage includes walls, stairwells, and mechanical space — the whole building envelope. Net (or usable) square footage is only the space you actually occupy: rooms, closets, hallways inside the unit. Residential listings almost always quote finished, heated living area , which excludes garages and unfinished basements. If you're comparing a real estate listing's square footage to your own room-by-room measurements, you're often comparing net to gross, and the numbers won't match — that's not a measuring error, it's a definitional one. Converting between units Square feet to square meters: multiply by 0.0929. So 200 sq ft × 0.0929 ≈ 18.58 m². Going the other way, multiply square meters by 10.7639. This matters if you're ordering material from a supplier that prices per square meter, or comparing a US listing to a metric one.
