H2: The Truth About COA Validity: What a Real Certificate of Analysis Tells You Every serious peptide buyer has heard of a Certificate of Analysis, or COA. Most suppliers provide one. But here is something that surprises many researchers: not all COAs are created equal, and some are not worth the PDF they are printed on. A valid, meaningful COA should include all of the following. Peptide sequence confirmation. The COA should state the full amino acid sequence as ordered and confirm it matches what was synthesized. Molecular weight verification. Mass spectrometry data should appear on the COA, showing the measured molecular weight of the peptide with a match to the theoretical value. Purity by HPLC. The COA should include an actual HPLC chromatogram or at minimum the numerical purity result expressed as a percentage. A COA that only states "greater than 95 percent" without instrument data to back it up is a red flag. Batch or lot number. A real COA is tied to a specific production batch. If you receive a COA with no lot number, you have no way of knowing if the document actually corresponds to the peptide in your vial. Date of analysis. Peptides can degrade. The analysis date tells you how current the quality data is. Analyst signature or laboratory identification. A legitimate COA includes documentation of who performed the analysis and where. H2: Contamination Risk in Imported Peptides: What Researchers Are Not Being Told Contamination is the issue that does not get discussed openly enough in the research peptide space. It is uncomfortable for suppliers to acknowledge and easy for buyers to overlook until it directly affects their results. Here is what you need to understand. Contamination in peptides comes from multiple sources. Incomplete deprotection during synthesis leaves protecting group residues in your final product. Inadequate purification leaves truncated sequences and deletion peptides alongside your target peptide. Poor handling during lyophilization can introduce microbial contamination. Environmental controls in the synthesis facility determine whether cross-contamination from other peptides occurs. Overseas manufacturing introduces additional contamination pathways. Long international shipping times expose peptides to temperature fluctuations that degrade sensitive compounds. Customs handling adds further risk. Repackaging by intermediaries, which happens more often than buyers realize, introduces handling contamination. Contamination is invisible without proper testing. You cannot smell, see, or feel a contaminated peptide. The vial looks exactly the same whether it is 99 percent pure or 85 percent pure with unidentified byproducts. Without HPLC and mass spectrometry verification tied to your specific batch, you are working blind. The research consequences are significant. Contaminated peptides produce off-target effects in assays. They generate inconsistent results between runs. They can damage cell lines used in your experiments. And in in vivo studies, peptide contaminants can confound your pharmacological data entirely, leading to conclusions that do not survive replication.
