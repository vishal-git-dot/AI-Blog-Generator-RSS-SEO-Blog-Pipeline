# 10K Sessions Of Gratitude - My First Open-Source Release

## Overview

I get this strange, delirious mix of envy and deep concern when I come here to dev.to, or consider sharing my work with someone who has the capability to judge it. But we all need to step out of our shell at some point, correct? You see, before agentic coding, IDE's like Cursor, and access to deep research assistants, my knowledge of coding was confined to SQL and HTML. It wasn't until I could sit with these tools that I could begin to grasp how to understand and build systems I could leverage to provide real value for my specific field of expertise - Music Metadata and Digital Rights Management. However, the rise of my engineering skillset, coincided with the adoption of the perpetually maligned "vibe-coder" - a title I am so concerned with being bestowed. Isaac Netero's 10,000 Punches In the anime Hunter X Hunter, there is a Martial Arts Master, Isaac Netero, who feels his skills are plateauing. As a result, he confines himself to the mountains, where he throws 10,000 punches a day in a radical act of gratitude towards the practice and art that made him. Having served in Operations and Research my entire career, in a very particular data-focused niche of the Music Industry, I felt very-much like an expert. I had risen to a point where understanding the data flow of massive XML packets between organizations transferring batches of audio was the minimum, half of my day was spent writing SQL queries, and the rest was spent writing tickets for Engineers. Despite all of that, I had plateau'd. I knew it made zero sense that the Music Industry ran on XML documents and prayers, but I couldn't write a solution to fix it. As it turns out, when my Research contract ended, I had quite a lot of time on my hands to try. So I went to the hills, Like Isaac Netero, and set about throwing my 10,000 punches a day. Critical Systems Thinking Replaces Jargon (Eventually) When I first sat down inside of an IDE and said okay, we're going to learn how to code, I was absolutely determined to ensure things were well thought out. I've worked in corporate long enough to know I cannot word vomit my way to a successful solution - I need to do research, plan out sprints, and up-skill. That all then begs the question, however - what am I building? I thought about my most recent work, which revolved mostly around these dreaded XML documents, which often contained incorrect metadata, which was often input by an annoyed employee like myself, forced to review 10,000 songs in a single .csv file. My mind eventually settled upon a clear question - what if we could make songs machine readable, so the literal audio can carry the information required by all stakeholders to ensure they are displayed, and paid properly. Now at the time, you would be fair in saying "hey homie, you don't even know what a .env file is, maybe you aren't the guy for this". My response would be "Absolutely Fair". If you asked me what a CI/CD pipeline is in college, I would have said one of my friends is probably protesting that this weekend. This is where I want to stress the 10,000 punches. This was not a problem I would solve in a day, nor it was something I could solve quickly. No matter what, if I keep showing up, humbly, learning, I will enhance my skillset, and leave this plateau. Approaching a technical problem, from a technical perspective, with expertise, is not "vibe"-anything in my mind. Regardless, beginning to code, even with the assistance of LLM's, clear sprint plans, and well researched solutions was deeply frustrating. In one sense, I was very much a novice, bumbling around where I didn't belong. In another sense, I was Isaac Netero - an expert at their craft, recognizing that rededicating yourself to a skill with grace requires deep patience. The result of that patience, and maybe not 10,000 commits, but 140 or so, is an audio protocol that I feel represents the depth and absolute limit of my knowledge and ability. It's called the Origin-Based Identity & Rights Transfer Protocol, or ORBIT . pls give us a look or star :) - github.com/focusjordan/ORBIT Using the Protocol, any stakeholder can replace a 10KB XML file with a highly compact CBOR payload , which is signed with cryptographic proof, and embedded directly into an audio signal as an imperceptible watermark. Beyond just this provenance solution, we've released a number of npm packages that solve for AI Music Detection, DSP Analysis, and Enhanced Music Metadata tagging. Plus, we've licensed the entire system under Apache 2.0. Reps, Reps, Reps I'll try to post more specific snippets about our work, about the code, and models we utilized, in a later post. For now, I just wanted to emphasize that the often arbitrary battlegrounds around jargon and the linguistics of Eng can sometimes feel like a big hurdle to actually sharing contributions. And if you are someone on here, who maybe took an asymmetric path like me to publishing your first packages, well the work is still super valuable. If you approach your problems with critical domain expertise, and if you treat your IDE like a practice — showing up every single day to throw your punches — you will inevitably build something meaningful.
